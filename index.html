A little about me

I grew up in Chandler, Arizona with a deep curiosity about two things: how the brain works, and how computers can be made to mimic it. That intersection led me to build machine learning pipelines trained on real colorectal cancer patient data at the ASU Biodesign Institute, model neural dynamics with differential equations at ASU's math department, and analyze protein folding data for tumor biomarkers at the College of Health Solutions.

I co-founded Aztecs Who Code, which started as a school club and grew into a city-wide initiative with coding bootcamps at the Chandler Sunset Library, where I taught more than 50 students to build and deploy their first websites. As a National Student Ambassador for the Mark Cuban Foundation, I expanded AI literacy programs across the country and spoke before the Arizona State Board of Education as the state's April Student of the Month.

This fall I head to Cal Poly San Luis Obispo to study Computer Science. I am most excited about problems at the edge of AI and biology: building models that actually change what a clinician can do for a patient, and ensuring the next generation has the tools to do the same.

ASU Biodesign Institute
Using AI to Predict Colorectal Cancer Treatments
Independently designed and built a machine learning pipeline trained on 1,107 real de-identified colorectal cancer patients from the GENIE BPC CRC dataset to tackle three clinical problems: predicting patient survival, recommending optimal chemotherapy, and identifying bacterial therapy candidates.
  • Random Forest classifier with 5-fold cross-validation: AUC 0.68 vs. 0.50 baseline, 63.9% accuracy.
  • Cancer stage, age, and tumor mutation count were the top three predictive features.
  • FOLFOX achieved the highest Stage IV median survival at 30.1 months.
  • Rule-based scoring flagged 72% of patients as strong bacterial therapy candidates.
  • Presented at AzSEF 2026 and the DataPaths national educator webinar.
